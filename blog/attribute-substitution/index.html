Attribute Substitution

December 26, 2017
Filed under Thinking, Fast and Slow

Attribute substitution occurs when an individual has to make a judgment (of a target attribute) that is computationally complex, and instead substitutes a more easily calculated heuristic attribute. source wikipedia

I came across this revolutionary concept, like infinitely many others, in the life changing book Thinking, Fast and Slow written by our lord and savior Daniel Kahneman, Danny Kanny.

If you haven’t read this book you should drop everything that you’re doing and start reading it right away. It is extremely well written and easy to read, with short chapters and lucid Hemingway-styled language lacking any pretentiousness. While I’m tempted to summarize the book I am bound to fail miserably as neither do I fully understand it, nor do I have the eloquence and skill necessary to undertake such an enormous writing project. Instead I’ve decided to write about the things that I myself am doing based on the wisdom acquired from this book.

Danny Kanny posits a two System model for our minds. The faster system which is also the system truly in charge, Mr. System 1, has a lot of tasks, one of them being to make things appear mundane. It is the primary reason why every morning upon waking up you don’t fall into an existential crisis, or when you see a flight of stairs you don’t start pondering about their true meaning but you simply put one foot in front of the other and get on with it. It is in many ways the biggest refuter of Existentialism, Sartre’s nemesis.

It does this by constantly forming a coherent story based on the information your senses are providing it and the information stored in your memory. However, being what we are today, we face questions in everyday life which have no answers. The amount of information in the world is so vast that it is impossible to form a coherent story but Mr. System 1 hasn’t evolved to deal with this and it insists on forming one, thus leading us astray.

The most obvious syndrome of this is attribute substitution. As Danny Kanny puts it, we replace the hard questions which cannot be answered, by easy ones that we know the answers to thereby deceiving ourselves into believing that we’ve found the answers.

For example, when we think about whether a person is guilty in a particular crime (a very difficult question) Mr. System 1 replaces it with whether the person is well-behaved and likely to have committed the crime (an easy question), our legal system tries to mitigate the influence of Mr. System 1 but only partially succeeds. On a more personal note, when faced with questions like what to do with your life or what will bring you great joy and happiness (very difficult questions) some people replace it with what is it you love to do and others with what will earn you a lot of money (both easy questions to answer). These substituted questions are very similar to the original questions and Mr. System 1 is a great expert at making us believe that we’ve answered the harder questions when we’ve actually answered the easier ones.

How to escape the clutches of Mr. System 1? This is what I’ve started doing, whenever I’m faced with a question that does not need to be answered immediately but which might have a large effect on my life

I write the question down somewhere permanent, like in a diary, and make a note of the statistical data (even if I’ll never be able to accumulate it precisely) that is pertinent to this question.

Statistics is Mr. System 1’s biggest nemesis. Here’s a very recent example. Right now I’m faced with the question of whether I should do a postdoc or not after grad school. I do not know the answer to this, but check this out: I have not found a single successful professor at any university in the US or Europe who was a math graduate student at JHU. This is just one piece, there’s also the question of the number of years a topology researcher spends as a postdoc, of whether a post-postdoc life is actually better than the lives of those who don’t do one, what is the number of topology researchers who drop out of academia after one or more postdocs (by my current estimate this number is close to 90%, and even higher in homotopy theory, but I need to do more research).

My gut is screaming at me saying that everybody is unique and that statistics cannot be used to make personal decisions or that the academics scene is different now and you should do whatever you like, but none of these are answers to the original question. That’s Mr. System 1 for you, it’ll do whatever it takes to find an answer even if the answer is not to the question that was asked. The only way to tame it is to think slow.
