Week 2 In the second week we'll begin with the study of knots.
  1. Notes on Tricolorability of knots and Linking number. Here is the link to the video about knots we saw today. There are too many problems in the notes, pick any random problem and try to solve it as the problems do not depend on each other. Here is a better table of knots.
  2. Notes on Alexander - Conway Polynomial. Compute the Conway polynomials for the following knots and links: 221 (Hopf link), 31 (trefoil), 421, 4 1 (figure eight), 51, 52, 521. Find the polynomials in this order so that you can use the earlier computations recursively. (The rest of the notes are here).
  3. Notes on Braids. Solve the relevant problems from sections 5-7. Section 4 is about Braid groups and is also very interesting. Try computing the braid groups for the knots 71, 91.
  4. We'll change gears now and look at an elementary theorem that goes by the funny name Ham Sandwich Theorem, here's a video of the proof. This is a special case of a deeper theorem called the Borsuk Ulam Theorem.
For further reading: Similar to the Alexander polynomial is a much stronger polynomial called the Jones polynomial, here are some notes on it.
 
Week 1 In the first week we'll be studying surfaces.
  1. These are the notes for the first two days. For the first day solve up to problems 2.22 on page 9. The goal of the first day is to get familiar with gluing diagrams. Here is the link for the YouTube video we saw in the class.
  2. From the same notes as yesterday solve problems from Section 3. Tomorrow we'll be moving on to different stuff, so please make sure you understand this section well and ask any questions and get things clarified.
    There are quite a few connected sum animations at this site. See especially these: 1, 2, 3, 4 (my apologies in advance for the background music).
  3. Here are the notes on Euler Characteristic. The wiki page of Platonic solids has details of the other geometric proof.
  4. Notes on Algebraic Classification of Surfaces. Make sure you understand the idea of the classification statement, being able to describe spaces using quantifiable algebraic objects is the central philosophy of algebraic topology. We had a nice discussion on infinity in the class today, here are some links on the same: Cantor's Diagonalizability Argument, Continuum Hypothesis, Ordinal Numbers, Cardinal Numbers.
For further reading: The theoretical basis for topology lies in Point Set Topology. See these notes for an introduction to it. This online course provides a very thorough and systematic treatement of the material that we cover in the first week.
