What's the deal with Curvature?
Filed under Gaussian Curvature, April 8, 2018.

When we think of curves we naturally think of them as being curved. A circle has some curvature where as a line has no curvature. Similarly for surfaces, a sphere is curved (and so is a cylinder) but a plane is not.

But there is a subtle difference between the way in which curves and surfaces are curved. It is possible to take a string and form a circle out of it without stretching or compressing, but it is not possible to take a flat piece of paper and shape it into a sphere (but you can into a cylinder) without stretching or compressing.

Hmmm.


A curve is any smooth 1-dimensional object with no sharp points or self-intersections. If we agree that lines have no curvature and circles have constant curvature then we’re more or less forced to say that curvature of a circle should be inversely proportional to the radius, after all a line is a circle with an infinite radius. We might as well

Define the curvature \(\kappa\) at every point of a circle of radius \(r\) to be \(1/r\) .

For more general curves, for example, a sine curve, different points should have different curvatures. As we know what the curvature of a circle is, the standard trick in maths is to take an arbitrary curve \(C\) and at each point \(p \in C\) try to find the circle that approximates it the best, the best fitting circle (apparently called an osculating circle). But how to find such a circle? And how would we know we’ve found the best fit?

Enter Calculus.
