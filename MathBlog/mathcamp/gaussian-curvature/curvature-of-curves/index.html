Curvature of Curves
Filed under Gaussian Curvature, April 8, 2018.

Consider a circle \(C_r\) of radius \(r\) , say \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\) . Then we shift \(C_r\) up so that \((0,0)\) is now on the circle and the equation becomes \(x^2 + (y - r)^2 = r^2\) . Around the point \((0,0)\) , \(C\) can be described as the graph of $$y = r - \sqrt{r^2 - x^2}$$

Expanding this out using the Taylor series we get

$$\begin{align} y &= r - \sqrt{r^2 - x^2} \\ &= r - r\sqrt{1 - (x/r)^2} \\ &\approx \dfrac{x^2}{2r} + \dfrac{x^4}{8r^3} + \cdots \end{align}$$ Notice that the curvature \(\kappa = 1/r\) is exactly the coefficient of \(x^2/2\) in the Taylor expansion, which is exactly \(y''(0)\) .

Suppose now \(C\) was the graph of a function \(y=f(x)\) with \(y(0) = 0\) and we were interested in finding the curvature of \(C\) at \((0,0)\) . If we further have \(f'(0) = 0\) then the Taylor series expansion of \(f(x) \) around 0 will look like \(f(x) \approx f''(0)\dfrac{x^2}{2} + \cdots\) . If \(C_r\) was the approximating circle of of \(C\) at \((0,0)\) then there Taylor series approximations should agree and we get the first definition of curvature,

If \(C\) is the graph \(y = f(x)\) and \(f(0) = 0 = f'(0)\) then the the curvature at \(p=(0,0) \in C\) is defined as $$\kappa(p) = f''(0)$$

Even though this definition requires several conditions on the curve, by rotating and translating the ambient \(\mathbb{R}^2\) (which should not change the curvature) we can change any \(C\) to be in the above form.

For example, if \(y=x^2\) we can see that the circle of radius \(1/y''(0) = 0.5\) best fits the parabola at \((0,0)\)

Q. Redefine curvature in terms of the best approximating parabola instead of a circle.

Q. Use this definition to find the curvature of the Sine Curve at it’s maxima, minima, and the points where it intersects the x-axis.

Curvature as Acceleration

Here is another method inspired from physics.

If a particle goes around in a circle with constant speed 1 then the acceleration is pointed towards the center and has value \(1/r\) which is exactly the curvature! More concretely, consider the circle \(C_r\) of radius \(r\) around the origin. We can parametrize \(C_r\) as $$c(t) = (r \cos (\omega t), r \sin (\omega t))$$

where the angular velocity \(\omega\) is a constant. We have \(|| c'(t) || = r \omega\) so that for \(\omega = 1/r\) i.e. $$c(t) = (r \cos (t/r), r \sin (t/r))$$ the path has constant speed 1. For this particular \(\omega\) by we get, $$||c''(t)|| = ||(r^{-1} \cos (t/r), r^{-1} \sin (t/r))|| = 1/r$$ which is exactly the curvature of \(C_r\) !!!

And now we make a leap. If \(C\) is any curve and \(C_r\) is the circle that best approximates \(C\) at \(p \in C\) then if we were to move along \(C\) with unit speed then the acceleration at \(p\) should be \(1/r\) .

Theorem. Let \(c: [a,b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n\) be a arbitrary curve. If for all \(t \in (a,b)\) the speed \(||c'(t)||\) equals 1 then the curvature at the point \(p=c(t)\) is $$\kappa(p) = ||c''(t)||$$

Q. Prove this theorem.

This theorem is useful if we’re able to find a unit speed parametrization (also called arc-length parametrization) of the curve. Unfortunately, this is not always easy, try finding one for the Sine Curve.

The Formula

Instead, here is a magical fact:

Theorem. For a curve \(c: [a,b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{3}\) such that \(||c'(t)|| \neq 0\) the curvature at \(c(t)\) is given by $$\kappa(t) = \dfrac{|| c'(t) \times c''(t)||}{||c'(t)||^3}$$

Q. Show that if for all \(t\) the speed \(||c'(t)|| = 1\) then \(c'(t) \perp c''(t)\) and so this agrees with the previous definition.

Q. Verify this formula for the circle \(C_r\) with parametrization \(c(t) = (r \cos (\omega(t)), r \sin (\omega(t)))\) where \(\omega\) is now an arbitrary function of \(t\) .

The derivation of this formula is a bit messy. Instead an easy way to understand what it is saying is to note that it is equivalent to the following $$\kappa = \dfrac{ \mbox{ component of acceleration that is normal to the curve}}{\mbox{speed}^2}$$

To conclude

Curvature should be thought of as a second order approximation of a curve, in much the same way that the slope of the tangent is the first order approximation. The fact that curvature is second order makes it non-linear and hence curvature data is much more interesting than tangential data. There are many more ways to interpret curvature, for example, as rate of change of length of a curve when the translated in the perpendicular direction [see Pg.5 in these slides] and curvature is really the first non-trivial geometric invariant.
